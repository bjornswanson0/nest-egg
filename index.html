    How the math works
    • Everything compounds monthly; contributions land before growth each month.
    • Employer match is applied per paycheck up to your match cap — no year-end true-up. Match dollars are always pre-tax.
    • Taxes: pre-tax 401(k) dollars (including the match) are discounted by your "tax rate in retirement" in every spendable figure; Roth and HSA dollars are treated as tax-free, and brokerage gains aren't taxed (a simplification in your favor).
    • Social Security shrinks the nest egg you need: the goal covers spending minus your benefit, plus a bridge (benefit × years) if you retire before it starts. The benefit is assumed to keep pace with inflation.
    • Annual contribution limits are indexed to your inflation assumption and include catch-ups: +$8,000 (401(k)) and +$1,100 (IRA) from age 50, +$1,000 (HSA) from 55. Blocked dollars keep working elsewhere.
    • When "grow my monthly contributions with raises" is on, flat monthly amounts (Roth, HSA, HYSA, brokerage, extra debt) rise with your income so your savings rate holds steady instead of quietly decaying for decades.
    • Extra debt payments go to the highest-APR balance first (avalanche). When a debt is paid off, its old minimum payment rolls into your plan instead of disappearing. Card minimums are modeled as fixed dollars — real card minimums shrink with the balance, so payoff runs slightly faster here. Income-driven student-loan plans aren't modeled — enter your actual monthly payment as the minimum.
    • The recommended order re-allocates the same monthly budget: employer match → debt at or above your high-interest threshold → emergency fund → HSA → Roth IRA → 401(k) to the annual max → remaining debt → taxable brokerage. Your plan and the recommended plan always spend identical dollars, so the gap between the two lines is pure ordering.
    • The shaded band runs your exact plan with every investment return 2 points higher and lower — a reminder that the smooth line is the middle of a wide range, not a promise.
    • Roth IRA income limits aren't enforced — at high incomes the model assumes you'd use the backdoor. The HSA's payroll FICA advantage isn't counted (it's slightly better than shown).

    This is an educational tool, not financial advice. Numbers are projections built on your assumptions — real markets won't cooperate so smoothly.
